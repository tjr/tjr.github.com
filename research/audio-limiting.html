Audio Limiting

some research notes prepared for the SFFC Sound Team by Trevis Rothwell

Problem Definition

The problem we are trying to solve here is that of, SFFC audio recordings are coming out much too quiet overall, both in an absolute sense (needing to crank the volume on the car stereo system to ridiculous levels) and when listened to side-by-side with comparable recordings from other ministries.

We want to increase the level of SFFC audio recordings without going overboard and introducing digital clipping.

Background Information

In the ye olde days of recording, everything went straight to a wax disc which was then duplicated into vinyl discs. More familiar was a slightly later process, in which the recording was done first to analog magnetic tape, and then "mastered" to a wax disc for making duplicates. These wax discs were very sensitive to excess sound levels, and could easily be ruined if the sound level got too high (much like digital clipping today). Analog tape, however, was much more flexible in this regard; too high of a sound would start to cause over-saturation and eventually distortion, but it was a gradual process, and a little bit of excess sound level was generally acceptable.

To reduce the possibility of the analog tape output signal getting too high for the wax disc, a sound engineer would sit at a mixing console in between the tape playback and the disc recorder, monitor the sound levels, and turn the level of the tape output down if it got too loud for the disc. This monotonous job was eventually replaced with technology in the form of a limiter device, which did the same thing automatically, but was able to do it much faster and more reliably.

In addition to not ruining the wax discs with sound overload, this allowed the possibility to create master discs that were overall much louder and stronger in sound than was done previously; the level of the tape output could be turned up so that quieter passages were more audible, while the limiter made sure that louder passages did not get too loud. The entire recording could be brought closer to the maximum volume permitted by the wax discs, without exceeding it.
