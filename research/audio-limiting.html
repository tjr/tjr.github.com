Audio Limiting

some research notes prepared for the SFFC Sound Team by Trevis Rothwell

Problem Definition

The problem we are trying to solve here is that of, SFFC audio recordings are coming out much too quiet overall, both in an absolute sense (needing to crank the volume on the car stereo system to ridiculous levels) and when listened to side-by-side with comparable recordings from other ministries.

We want to increase the level of SFFC audio recordings without going overboard and introducing digital clipping.

Where Are We At?

Here is a graphical representation of a wave file from a recent SFFC service, prepared using Audacity:

[First, a few words about what exactly we're seeing. The waveform view in Audacity shows us the amplitude of the waveform on a scale from 0.0 to +/- 1.0, where 0.0 is silent, and +/- 1.0 is as far away from silent as the sound can get without clipping.]

On Audacity's scale, we can see that the majority of the wave hovers around an amplitude of 0.3 or 0.4, with occasional bursts past 0.5. (The strong wave elements at the beginning and end are the musical intro/outro tags, which appear to be more solidly around 0.5.)

Where Do We Want to Go?

For comparison, here is a graphical representation of a wave file from the 2009 ministers' conference hosted by Kenneth Copeland Ministries:

We see that this audio wave peaks out around 0.9 on the Audacity scale, and is frequently right up at that peak; the peaks aren't random bursts, and rarely does the wave drop below 0.5.

Just Raise the Amplitude

Do we need to bother with a limiter? The original clip was quite low. We could probably just increase the recording level without introducing a limiter and see an improvement toward fixing the problem.

For comparison, here is the clip just boosted by approximately 8dB, with no limiter:

Definitely an overall strong clip, but there's a lot of variance in the waveform level, including some high spots that are probably clipping. So maybe 8dB is too much; we could boost it by 6dB, perhaps, and get a stronger waveform without clipping. Having the limiter in place, though, would help us to maximize the level without going too high.

Tools Used

For this work, I used Pro Tools 10 to process the audio, specifically using the standard compressor/limiter plugin that comes with Pro Tools 10. A higher-quality limiter plugin might offer superior results, though for one track of spoken word audio maybe nothing noticeable.
