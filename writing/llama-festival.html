A Trip to the Iowa Youth Llama Festival

by Trevis Rothwell, updated in September 2011

Ever since playing the Infocom computer game Bureacracy What might the llama festival be like? I pictured in my mind something like a state fair or a carnaval: various food vendors, game booths, face painting, balloons, and llamas. Maybe the llamas would just be milling about with the people? That might be fun.

When we arrived in Eldora, we saw a small number of people accompanied by a surprisingly large number of llamas. No food vendors or game booths. There was some sort of run-down abandoned race track nearby, but most of the activity seemed to be taking place in a large barn.

The Llama Show

We quickly found that a llama show was going on in one of the barns, so we joined the audience and watched as Iowa youth showed their llamas. It was a lot like a dog show, only with llamas instead of dogs. The owners brought their llamas into the barn and walked around, then stopped and waited for judges to rate the appearance and behavior of the llamas.

The final event in the llama show was an opportunity for the especially young youth to show their llamas. As this concluded, one of the announcers said they were going to break for lunch. I got my hopes up for the corn dogs, but all I saw was people with sack lunches. Alas.

We wandered into the second barn where the many of the llamas were relaxing in pens. Eager photographer that I am, I just started taking pictures, but Jennifer was concerned that the llamas might not like that. (I was too excited photographing real, live llamas to be prudent.) We walked over to some llama owners and asked if they thought the llamas would mind being photographed. They said no, that this shouldn't bother them. (Whew.)

So we continued to walk around the barn, taking photographs of llamas. We came upon a llama that was wearing some sort of black body suit; I assumed that the llama was undergoing medical treatment of some sort. While taking pictures of a nearby llama, a man called out from amidst his lunch that we could pet it if we wanted to.

It turned out that this man and his family owned several of the llamas, including the one in the black body suit, and his twin daughters were youth contestants in the festival. It also turned out that there was more to the black body suit that met the eye.

The Costume Competition

As it turned out, this llama in the black body suit was not undergoing medical treatment, but rather was in preparatory stages of wearing a costume for the next competition at the festival. I had no idea llama costume competitions took place.

I'm still not entirely sure what this particular llama was supposed to be dressed up as, but I am quite certain that the other of their llamas in the contest was dressed up as Barbara Eden in I Dream of Jeannie. "Wait until we get the head-dress on!" I was instructed as I took a picture. The mother and one of the daughters started putting a bright pink gown onto the llama.

I asked if the llamas were bothered by having costumes on. "Oh yes, they hate it," our llama informant told us. He went on to explain that if the handlers can keep the llamas under control even when the animals are so very bothered by the costumes, then they must really be doing a good job. This effect is magnified when the llamas are wearing something covering their head, hence, I presume, the unexpected interest in llama head-dresses.

We thanked the family for sharing with us, and continued wandering around the barn. More and more llamas were being prepared for the costume competition. One was being disguised as a scarecrow ("Wait until we get the head-dress on!" I was instructed again, while taking a photograph of the partially-disguised llama), another as science fiction villian Darth Vader, and another as a clown (although the expression on this particular llama's face suggested to us that he was too dignified for this, refusing to accept the fact that he was, indeed, dressed up as a clown).

As the costume contest was about to begin, I waited outside in between the two barns, to get good closeup shots of the llamas as they walked from the "backstage" barn to the "competition hall". As I got a picture of the Darth Vader llama, the llama, who likely couldn't see very well, jerked his head in the general direction of my very quiet-but-evidently-not-quiet-enough digital camera. His young handler also looked in my direction, and didn't appear particularly happy. I hoped that I hadn't messed up the llama's concentration, and quickly ducked back into the main barn.

And so the costume competition began. We saw more great llama costumes, and along with them, their handlers were also wearing costumes that went along with whatever the llamas were were wearing.

The costume segment ended all too soon, but it wasn't the end of the festival. While I was hanging out in between the two barns before the costume competition, I met an older man who was rummaging around in a pile of bars and pipes leaning up against a wall. He explained that he had been involved with llamas for a number of years, and asked how I heard about the festival. I told him about the newspaper article, and offered to help him with whatever he was doing with the bars and pipes. He declined. I shortly came to find out that he was assembling equipment for the llama obstacle course.

Llamas Hate Water...

...so naturally part of the obstacle course included leading the llamas through a small pool of water. Other aspects of the obstacle course included a bar to jump over, a hoop to jump through, and a tunnel with sparkly strands of sparkly stuff covering the entrance and exit. (Llamas also hate sparkly stuff.)

After watching a fair bit of the obstacle course, we decided that we had seen enough llamas, took enough photographs, and it was time to go. Before we left, I thanked the man who had told us so much about the llamas. He warned me, "You're going to miss the best part of the obstacle course, when we bring out the teeter-totters!" In retrospect, I wish we had stayed to see that, but oh well.

Maybe next year.
