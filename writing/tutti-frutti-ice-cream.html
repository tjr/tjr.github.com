In Search of Tutti Frutti Ice Cream

Home : Writing : by Trevis Rothwell, last updated in July 2012

For over twenty years, I have been on a quest to find tutti frutti ice cream, a flavor which has for the most part fallen off the face of the earth. I am sharing what I have discovered with the hope that it will be helpful to others on a similar search.

Motivation and Backstory

Around 1989, my family and I saw a rerun of The Adventures of Ozzie and Harriet in which Ozzie desperately searches for tutti frutti ice cream. In the 1950's, tutti frutti ice cream was perhaps at its peak popularity, and Ozzie managed to locate it inside of twelve hours or so (contained within a 30-minute broadcast, of course).

I had never tasted tutti frutti ice cream. I could only imagine what it might be like based on the descriptions in the television show. But watching that made me want to find some tutti frutti also.

As of July 2012, I have yet to obtain any.

Origins of Tutti Frutti Ice Cream

The invention of tutti frutti ice cream has been attributed to Roy Motherhead, who ran an ice cream business in Okolona, Kentucky through the late 1950's. While "tutti frutti" is Italian for "all fruit", and would make a good name for a fruit-flavored ice cream, the flavor's name was derived from Motherhead's daughter, who was nicknamed "Toodie".

My Findings

Shortly after watching the Ozzie and Harriet episode, we began looking for tutti frutti ice cream in the area grocery stores around Dallas, Texas, not turning up anything. A local ice cream manufacturer offered to make us some tutti frutti ice cream, but they would have to make it custom just for us, and we would need to buy five gallons of it. We declined, thinking that five gallons would be way too much, and we'd surely come across tutti frutti in smaller quantities if we kept looking.

We kept looking, but did not find it. We moved to other cities across the central United States, and every trip to a grocery store included an obligatory scan of the ice cream section in hopes of seeing a container of tutti frutti.

In late 1998, I happened to stumble upon some evidence that tutti frutti was a flavor popular in India, and started contacting Indian restaurants and grocery stores to see if they carried it. One Indian grocer in St. Louis explained that he used to regularly stock it in his store, but due to lack of demand, he had just stopped ordering it. However, he would be happy to place a special order of five gallons, provided we bought it all. Contary to our experience in Dallas, this time we agreed.

It turned out that this ice cream was in fact labeled as "tooty fruity", a mispelling which makes me wonder if it was authentic. Maybe it was, maybe it wasn't, but I simply could not accept it as a successful find. For what it's worth, worth, the "tooty fruity" had a rather non-descript-flavored ice cream base, with bits of candied fruit. It seemed that most of the overall flavor came from the bits of fruit rather than the ice cream itself, and tasted not unlike a combination of orange "Dreamsicle" ice cream bars and dried Turkish apricots.

By now the dotCom boom was coming into full swing, and web search engines was making finding anything in the world easier than it used to be. I came to discover that tutti frutti was still a moderately popular flavor in Massachusetts, though perhaps not sold under that name.

Gary, of Gary's Ice Cream in Chelmsford, Massachusetts, states that a flavor called "frozen pudding" is the same as tutti frutti, only without nuts. (Tutti frutti includes nuts, usually walnuts, Gary says.) Gary's web page describes frozen pudding as "raisins, pineapples, maraschino cherries, apples, and peaches soaked in Meyers dark rum".

I found this out just months after a trip to Massachusetts. My next trip there didn't occur for a number of years, but during a trip to Boston in the fall of 2008, I continued my search. The popular Cambridge ice cream parlor Toscanini's didn't have it. Brigham's, an ice cream manufacturer that provides containers of ice cream for grocery stores and operates a number of eat-in ice cream parlors, claims they make frozen pudding from September through December. When I stopped in at the Brigham's parlor near the Free Software Foundation in Boston, the rather grim clerk at the counter said, "It's seasonal; we don't have any." This was October. [That Brigham's location has since closed. Coincidence? Probably.]

I asked a native Boston tour guide about frozen pudding. He said that some guy in his neighborhood makes it, but just closed up shop for the winter. I spent $40 in taxi fare going to grocery stores in search of it. Nothing. I tried several other ice cream parlors; only one person laughed at me. I did end up getting some tasty butter pecan ice cream at a shop in Harvard Square, but it just wasn't what I was after.

I headed back home at the end of October, but only a few months later, Boston Food & Whine published a review of Brigham's frozen pudding! And the reviewer didn't even want to taste it!

In January of 2012, I was in the Boston area again, but too busy to do ice cream searching outside of my immediate area. I did check with all of the ice cream parlors I could locate in and around Cambridge, but none of them were making frozen pudding at that time.

Tutti Frutti Recipes

The rather authoritative-looking textbook Ice Cream offers the following recipe:

Tutti-frutti. To 9 gal plain [ice cream] mix add 4-8 qt of a mixture of several fruits of a suitable mixture for tutti-frutti. A desireable mixture consists of the following fruits chopped into small pieces: 2 qt red cherries, 2 qt green pineapple, 2 lb raisins, 2 qt crushed pineapple, 2 lb nuts. Color light pink with red coloring.
In the Ozzie & Harriet episode, tutti frutti was described in song as containing peaches, pineapples, and cherries. This recipe has no mention of peaches, and does include rasins. However, we should consider the possibility that the words of the song were written to be more lyrical than academic, and that it is not in fact an actual recipe.

In any event, the above recipe suggests that the flavor would come predominantly from the bits of fruit rather than the ice cream base, and it seems overall in line with what we learned from Gary.

A web search for tutti frutti ice cream can turn up lots more recipes, the contents of which can vary wildly. Are any one of these recipes more authentic than the one listed above? I have no idea. It would be awesome to find a recipe for tutti frutti that was actually published circa the 1950's...

Frozen Pudding Recipes

Recipes for frozen pudding seem to predate Roy Motherhead's creation of tutti frutti by decades. For example, the following recipe was found in The Ice Book by Thomas Masters, published in London in 1844:

NESSELRODE, OR FROZEN PUDDING.

Take one pint of cream, half a pint of milk, the yolks of four eggs, one ounce of sweet almonds pounded, and half a pound of sugar; put them in a stewpan on a gentle fire, set it as thin as custard; when cold, add two wine-glasses of brandy and two wine-glasses of nectar, (a delicious beverage, prepared only by the author.) Freeze, and when sufficiently congealed, add one pound of preserved fruits, with a few currants; cut the fruit small, and mix well with the ice, and put it into moulds, and immerse them in a freezing mixture, such as ice and salt, &c, until required for table.

This doesn't appear to be exactly the same as the modern-day recipe (what is this nectar, "prepared only by the author"?), but it certainly bears a resemblance to what we know of as frozen pudding / tutti frutti. In 1909, Mrs. B. B. Cutter offered the following in her book Practical Recipes:

FROZEN PUDDING

1 pint of rick milk,
2 cups sugar,
1 cup of boiled rice,
2 tablespoons of gelatine,
1 quart of very thick cream,
1 pound of candied cherries,
4 tablespoons of good sherry.

Boil the milk and thicken with rice, stirring constantly while boiling for 15 minutes; add gelatine while hot, permit it to cool before adding the sherry. Freeze 10 minutes before adding fruit, which you stir thoroughly, then add the cream whipped stiff. Turn out on a glass dish as you would serve ice cream.

This recipe includes only cherries as fruit, and substitutes a variety of other ingredients.

The 1844 recipe for frozen pudding also uses the name Nesselrode. A modicum of web searching turns up that Nesselrode Pie is a historic and virtually extinct dessert originally made with candied chestnuts and candied fruits. This recipe recommends a using a 10-ounce jar of Raffetto's Nesselro fruits, but advises that if you cannot find that, you could subsitute a mix of candied fruit, "tutti fruiti".

Is Nesselrode Pie perhaps the ancient ancestor of tutti frutti ice cream? More information is clearly needed, but I have a developing theory: Nesselrode Pie and related desserts were brought over to New England from Europe, where it eventually morphed into frozen pudding ice cream. Independently, Roy Motherhead invented tutti frutti ice cream, which ended up being similar in concept to frozen pudding. (Pure speculation: perhaps Motherhead even independently based tutti frutti ice cream on an experience with Nesselrode Pie?) Over the following decades, frozen pudding ice cream and tutti frutti ice cream merged into one largely indistinguishable flavor.

If any readers have further insight into the lineage of Nesselrode Pie, please feel free to send me an email. My theory is largely unsubstantiated at the moment, but it seems plausible. I need more culinary anthropology data!

[Serendipitously, Nesselrode Pie was once featured on another television classic, an episode of the Andy Griffith Show! This particular recipe seems to have lost the bits of candied fruit, but nevertheless it is an unexpected and almost certainly unintended dessert linkage between the two programs, and apparently the catalyst for a brief quest for some viewers.]

Tutti Frutti Quotes

Here are a few quotes describing tutti frutti / frozen pudding:
  • "Well, it's kind of hard to explain." (Ozzie Nelson, in the tutti frutti episode)
  • "We wonder about the origination of frozen pudding as we can't figure out why anyone would enjoy this combination. A strangely flavored ice cream base with bits of unidentifiable fruit. Didn't this used to be called tutti-frutti?" (from a review of Dean Dairy's ice cream products)
  • "...with peaches and pineapples, too, and cherries sprinkled through..." (from a song in the tutti frutti episode)
